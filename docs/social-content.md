### LINKEDIN POST 1 — Aesthetic nurse prescriber hiring

Most aesthetic clinic listings lose the candidate before they've read past the second line.

The problem isn't where you're posting. It's what you're posting.

Aesthetic nurse prescribers — the NMC-registered, V300-qualified practitioners that every aesthetic clinic wants — receive multiple approaches per week. They have leverage, and they know it.

When I look at the listings that get applications, they consistently do four things:

1. State the salary range (even a wide one). Listings without a range get 40–60% fewer applications. Candidates assume the worst and move on.

2. Name the indemnity arrangement. Annual MDDUS/MDU/Hamilton Fraser premiums run to £2,000–£5,000+ for prescribers. Whether you provide it or require self-funding is a significant financial consideration. Say which.

3. List the CPD budget. £1,500–3,000/year is standard at practices that retain good practitioners. If you fund it, state it.

4. Describe clinical autonomy. "You have full autonomy over treatment decisions, including the right to decline cases." Experienced prescribers read that and think: finally.

The clinics filling aesthetic nurse roles fast aren't offering more money. They're writing better listings.

### LINKEDIN POST 3 — What candidates want

Here's what I've learned from looking at what makes some private practice job listings work and others sit empty:

Most listings describe the job. The best listings describe the opportunity.

The distinction matters because experienced clinical professionals in UK private practice — aesthetic nurse prescribers, RCVS-registered vets, HCPC physiotherapists, GOC optometrists — are not desperate for work. They have options. Your listing needs to give them a reason to choose you.

The factors that consistently drive applications:

CPD investment. Not "we support professional development." An annual figure. £2,000 CPD budget means something. "We support development" means nothing.

Indemnity. Whether provided or self-funded affects take-home pay materially. State it.

Clinical autonomy. The single biggest reason experienced clinicians leave their current role is clinical frustration. If your practice genuinely gives practitioners freedom over their decisions — including the ability to decline inappropriate treatment requests — say so explicitly.

Work environment. Appointment volume, equipment, patient demographic. Candidates want to visualise the working day before they apply.

None of these are expensive to offer. Some you already offer. You're just not writing them into your listings.

### LINKEDIN POST 4 — Veterinary staffing crisis

The UK has a structural veterinary workforce problem, and it is not going to resolve quickly.

Pre-Brexit, EEA-trained vets represented approximately 40–50% of new RCVS registrations annually. The combination of Brexit uncertainty from 2016 and formal implementation in 2021 significantly reduced that pipeline. UK veterinary schools cannot scale fast enough to fill the gap. The RCVS Workforce Action Plan, published in 2021 and updated since, identifies workforce availability as the single largest threat to UK veterinary service continuity.

Simultaneously, over 40% of UK vet practices are now owned by corporate groups — CVS, IVC Evidensia, VetPartners, Medivet. They have centralised benefits, formal CPD programmes, structured progression paths, and group indemnity arrangements. Independent practices competing on salary alone are fighting on unfavourable terms.

The practices winning the hiring battle in veterinary right now are competing on differentiation, not compensation. They're articulating what corporate ownership has eroded: genuine clinical autonomy, patient continuity, real input into practice decisions, and — for the right candidate — a pathway toward buy-in or partnership.

These are real advantages. Most independent practices are not stating them clearly in their job listings.

### LINKEDIN POST 5 — Complete hiring guide

Five-step process for hiring clinical staff in UK private practice without using an agency:

**1. Define the role precisely before writing anything.**
Employment type, regulatory body (NMC/RCVS/HCPC/GOC), prescribing requirement, scope, volume, rota, CQC supervision requirements. Getting this clear makes every subsequent step faster.

**2. Write a listing that sells the opportunity, not the job description.**
Lead with what makes your practice worth joining — clinical autonomy, CPD investment, equipment quality, culture. State the salary range. List the indemnity arrangement and CPD budget explicitly.

**3. Respond within 3–5 working days.**
Experienced clinical practitioners at this level are considering multiple opportunities simultaneously. A response time of more than a week loses candidates. Acknowledge every application. Move the good ones to interview immediately.

**4. Conduct an interview that both assesses and sells.**
Ask about clinical decision-making, complications management, CPD history. Then show them the practice: the treatment rooms, the equipment, the team. The best candidates are evaluating you as much as you are evaluating them.

**5. Make the offer fast.**
Verbal offer same-day. Written offer within 24 hours. Be prepared to negotiate on start date more than salary — a practitioner working their notice is a better signal than one available immediately.
